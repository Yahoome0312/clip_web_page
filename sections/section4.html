4. Limitations & Conclusion

4.1 Limitations

Despite its revolutionary capabilities, CLIP is not perfect. It faces several key challenges:

Fine-Grained Recognition

It struggles with specific details. E.g., distinguishing a "2016 Ford Mustang" from a "2018 Ford Mustang" or identifying specific flower species.

Abstract Concepts

It often fails at counting ("How many apples?") or complex spatial relationships ("Is the cup on or under the table?").

Prompt Sensitivity

A slight change in wording (e.g., "A dog" vs. "A photo of a dog") can swing accuracy by over 5%. This necessitates "Prompt Engineering".

1. What is the core training objective of CLIP?

Predicting the exact caption of an image word-by-word (Generative).
Matching images and text in a shared embedding space (Contrastive).
Classifying images into 1,000 fixed ImageNet categories.

2. Why is "Zero-Shot" classification possible with CLIP?

Because it was trained on every possible object in the universe.
Because it understands visual concepts via language, so labels can be arbitrary text.

3. What is a known limitation of CLIP?

It struggles with counting objects (e.g., "3 apples vs 5 apples").
It cannot recognize cartoons or sketches.

4.2 Conclusion: A Paradigm Shift

CLIP represents a fundamental shift from Closed-Set Classification to Open-World Understanding.

By aligning visual concepts with natural language on a massive scale (400M pairs), it gave computers a way to "understand" images in a way that feels much more human. It laid the groundwork for the generative AI revolution we see today with tools like DALL-E, Midjourney, and Stable Diffusion.
